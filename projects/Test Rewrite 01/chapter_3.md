## Chapter 3: Letting Go

**Scene 1: Marcus's mother opens the gara...**

**Scene 1: Marcus's mother opens the garage door searching for old photo albums and discovers Bruno in his makeshift den, leading to her terrified screams that bring Marcus running from the house.**

Marcus was sprawling across his bed, pretending to read his history textbook while actually scrolling through bear care websites on his phone, when he heard the garage door rumble open.

His entire body went rigid.

It was Tuesday. Mom wasn't supposed to go out there. She never went out there on Tuesdays. She worked late on Tuesdays.

He sat up, phone clattering to the floor, ears straining. Maybe she was just pulling the car in. Maybe she'd forgotten something. Maybe—

The scream shattered the afternoon quiet.

It wasn't a startled gasp or a surprised yelp. It was the kind of scream that came from somewhere primal and terrified, the sound of a person confronting something that shouldn't exist in their ordered world. It rose and rose, cutting through walls and windows, turning Marcus's blood to ice.

He was running before he consciously decided to move, taking the stairs three at a time, his socked feet sliding on the hardwood as he rounded the corner. The door to the garage stood wide open, and his mother's screams had dissolved into rapid, breathless gasps.

"Mom!" Marcus burst through the doorway.

The garage smelled wrong—he'd gotten so used to it that he'd stopped noticing, but now, seeing it through his mother's eyes, the reality crashed over him. The sharp tang of urine from the litter box he'd improvised. The sweet-rot smell of old fruit. The musky, wild scent of bear that clung to everything.

His mother stood frozen near the entrance, one hand pressed against her chest, the other gripping a cardboard box labeled "Photos 2010-2015" so tightly her knuckles had gone white. Her face was pale, eyes wide and unblinking, fixed on the far corner where Marcus had built Bruno's den.

Bruno had emerged from the nest of blankets and was standing on his hind legs, sniffing the air with obvious confusion. He'd grown so much in the past three weeks. He wasn't the tiny, mewling cub Marcus had found anymore. He was nearly forty pounds now, his paws disproportionately large, his claws visible even from across the garage. In the dim light filtering through the dusty window, his eyes caught and reflected the glow, twin amber coins in a dark face.

He looked wild. He looked dangerous.

He looked like exactly what he was: a bear that didn't belong in a suburban garage.

"Marcus." His mother's voice came out strangled, barely above a whisper. She hadn't moved. Hadn't looked away from Bruno. "Marcus, tell me that's not—tell me I'm seeing things. Tell me that's not—"

"Mom, it's okay." Marcus moved slowly, carefully, positioning himself between his mother and Bruno. His heart hammered against his ribs. "He's not dangerous. He won't hurt you. Just—just don't make any sudden movements."

"*Won't hurt me?*" The whisper exploded into something sharp and high. "Marcus, that's a *bear*. There's a *bear* in our garage. How—why—what have you—"

Bruno dropped to all fours and took a step forward, curious about the new person, the new sounds. He made a soft huffing noise that Marcus had learned meant he was interested, investigating.

His mother stumbled backward, the box of photos tumbling from her hands. Pictures scattered across the concrete floor—Marcus as a baby, family vacations, birthday parties, all the documented moments of a normal life. The life that was ending right now, in this moment.

"Stay back!" she shouted at Bruno, then to Marcus: "Make it stay back!"

"Bruno, no. Stay." Marcus used the firm voice he'd practiced, the one he'd seen trainers use in videos. To his relief, Bruno paused, tilting his head in that endearing way that made him look almost like a confused puppy.

Almost. But not quite. Never quite.

"You *named* it?" His mother's voice cracked. "Marcus, what have you done? How long has this been—three weeks? That smell, those noises at night—I thought it was raccoons. You said it was raccoons!"

"I know. I'm sorry. I can explain—"

"Explain? *Explain?*" She was backing toward the door now, her hand fumbling behind her for the frame. "We're calling the police. We're calling animal control. We're—oh my God, Marcus, do you have any idea how dangerous this is? How illegal? How completely—"

Her foot caught on one of the scattered photographs and she stumbled. Bruno, startled by the sudden movement, reared up again and let out a sound—not quite a growl, not quite a bark, but something in between that echoed off the garage walls.

His mother screamed again.

"Mom, please!" Marcus held up both hands, divided between them, protecting each from the other. "Just go inside. I'll handle this. I'll explain everything, I promise, just—please go inside."

She stared at him like she'd never seen him before, this son who had hidden a bear in their garage, who stood between her and a wild animal like it was the most natural thing in the world. Tears streamed down her face.

"I'm calling your father," she said, her voice shaking. "Don't you move. Don't you dare move."

She fled into the house, and Marcus heard the door slam, the lock click. Through the window that looked into the kitchen, he could see her grab her phone with trembling hands, could see her mouth moving rapidly as she spoke.

Marcus turned back to Bruno, who had settled onto his haunches, looking uncertain and afraid. The cub had picked up on the tension, the fear. His ears were flattened against his head.

"It's okay, buddy," Marcus whispered, but his own voice was shaking. "It's going to be okay."

But as he sank down onto the cold concrete floor, surrounded by scattered photographs of a childhood that felt like it belonged to someone else, Marcus knew nothing would ever be okay again.

**Scene 2: Marcus's father arrives home e...**

**Scene 2: Marcus's father arrives home early from his job site, and the family has an intense confrontation in the living room where Marcus must explain his actions while his parents express their fear, anger, and disappointment.**

The gravel crunched in the driveway twenty minutes later—a sound Marcus had been dreading. His father's truck door slammed with a force that seemed to shake the windows.

Marcus sat on the couch in the living room, his mother standing near the fireplace with her arms wrapped tightly around herself. She hadn't spoken to him since calling his father, just stood there staring at the framed family photos on the mantel like they belonged to strangers.

The front door burst open.

His father still wore his dusty work boots and the fluorescent safety vest from the construction site. Concrete dust streaked his jeans. He must have dropped everything and driven straight home—Marcus had never seen him leave work early before.

"Where is it?" His father's voice was low, controlled in a way that was somehow worse than yelling. "Where's the bear?"

"Garage," Marcus whispered. "In the old dog crate."

His father's jaw worked for a moment, then he strode past them toward the garage door. Marcus heard the door open, heard his father's sharp intake of breath, heard the door close again. When his father returned, his face had gone pale beneath his tan.

"Jesus, Marcus." He pulled off his hard hat and set it on the coffee table with shaking hands. "That's a *bear*. A wild animal. In our garage."

"He's not wild," Marcus said, then immediately regretted it.

"Not wild?" His mother's voice cracked. "Marcus, I thought—when I opened that crate and saw those *eyes*—I thought I was going to have a heart attack. Do you understand that? My own son put a *bear* in our garage."

His father sank into the armchair across from Marcus, rubbing his face with both hands. The living room felt too small suddenly, the walls pressing in. The familiar smell of his mother's lavender candles mixed with the dust from his father's clothes, creating something nauseating.

"Start from the beginning," his father said. "And don't leave anything out."

Marcus told them. About finding Bruno abandoned near the zoo's perimeter fence. About the research he'd done on bear cub care. About the formula and the heating pad and the careful feeding schedule. His voice sounded hollow in his own ears, each word landing like a stone in the thick silence.

"You stole from a zoo," his mother said when he finished. "You've been lying to us for *weeks*."

"I didn't steal—he was going to die. They left him there."

"That's not how it works!" His father's control finally broke. He stood up, pacing to the window and back. "You don't just *take* things because you think you know better. There are people—professionals—who handle these situations."

"The professionals left him to die!"

"You don't know that!" His father's voice rose. "You're thirteen years old, Marcus. You don't know what you're talking about. That animal could have killed you. Could have killed your mother. Do you understand the danger you put this family in?"

Marcus's throat tightened. "He wouldn't hurt anyone. He's just a baby."

"A baby bear is still a bear." His mother moved to stand beside his father, presenting a united front. "What were you thinking? What was your plan—to keep it forever? To hide a *bear* in our garage until it was full-grown?"

"I was thinking someone had to care!" The words burst out of Marcus before he could stop them. "Someone had to notice he existed!"

The silence that followed felt like a physical thing, pressing down on all of them.

His father stared at him. "What?"

Marcus's eyes burned. He looked down at his hands, at the small scratches on his wrists from Bruno's claws during feeding time. "Nothing. Forget it."

"No." His father's voice had changed, some of the anger draining away. "What did you mean by that?"

"I just—" Marcus's voice cracked. "He was all alone. Nobody saw him. Nobody cared that he was there, dying in the dirt. And I couldn't just—I couldn't just walk away."

His mother's expression shifted, confusion replacing some of the fear. "Marcus—"

"I'm sorry," Marcus said, the words tumbling out now. "I know it was wrong. I know I should have told someone. But he would have died, and nobody would have even known. Nobody would have cared."

"We would have cared," his father said quietly. "If you'd told us—"

"When?" The question came out sharper than Marcus intended. "When would I have told you? At dinner, which we haven't eaten together in three weeks? Or maybe when you got home at nine o'clock last night? Or the night before, when you didn't get home at all?"

His father flinched like he'd been slapped.

"Marcus," his mother said, her voice softer now but still strained. "That's not fair."

"Isn't it?" Marcus felt tears sliding down his cheeks now, hot and shameful. "I did my research. I was careful. I fed him every four hours. I kept him warm and safe. I *took care* of him."

"By lying to us," his mother said. "By sneaking around. By putting yourself in danger."

"I know." Marcus wiped his face with his sleeve. "I know it was wrong. But he's alive. Doesn't that count for something?"

His father sat back down, heavily, like someone had cut his strings. He looked older suddenly, the lines around his eyes deeper in the lamplight. Outside, the streetlights flickered on, casting long shadows across the carpet.

"You're grounded," his father said finally. "Indefinitely. No phone, no computer except for homework. You'll come straight home from school."

Marcus nodded. He'd expected as much.

"And tomorrow," his mother added, her voice still shaking, "you're calling the zoo. You're going to apologize and explain what happened. And then we're returning that animal to where it belongs."

The words hit Marcus like a punch to the chest. He'd known this was coming—had known from the moment his mother screamed in the garage—but hearing it said out loud made it real in a way that stole his breath.

"Can I—" His voice broke. "Can I say goodbye?"

His parents exchanged a long look. His mother's eyes were red-rimmed, her mascara smudged. His father's shoulders sagged with exhaustion.

"Tomorrow," his father said. "After we call the zoo. After we figure out what happens next."

Marcus nodded, not trusting himself to speak.

"Go to your room," his mother said. "We need to talk. And Marcus—" She waited until he looked at her. "We're glad you're safe. We're glad nothing happened to you. But this—this can't happen again. Do you understand?"

"Yes, ma'am."

Marcus climbed the stairs to his room, each step feeling like it took all his strength. Behind him, he heard his parents' voices, low and urgent. His father said something about calling animal control. His mother mentioned insurance, liability.

In his room, Marcus lay on his bed and stared at the ceiling. Through the floor, he could hear Bruno's soft grunting from the garage below. The sound that had become so familiar, so comforting over the past weeks.

Tomorrow, it would be gone.

Tomorrow, he would have to let go.

But tonight, through the heating vents, he could still hear Bruno breathing. Still know that the cub was warm and fed and safe.

For one more night, at least, neither of them was alone.

**Scene 3: Late that night, Marcus's father comes to his room**

Marcus lay in the dark, staring at the glow-in-the-dark stars he'd stuck to his ceiling three years ago. Most had lost their luminescence, leaving only faint ghosts of constellations that didn't quite exist. His eyes burned from crying, but the tears had finally stopped. His throat felt raw, like he'd swallowed sandpaper.

The house was silent except for the occasional creak of settling wood and the distant hum of the refrigerator. His mother had gone to bed an hour ago, her footsteps heavy on the stairs. Marcus had heard her pause outside his door, heard the soft intake of breath like she might knock, but then she'd moved on.

He didn't blame her for being scared. The look on her face when she'd stumbled backward from the garage, dropping the photo albums—he'd never forget that. Not anger. Pure terror.

A soft knock broke the silence.

Marcus didn't answer. He pulled his comforter higher, turning toward the wall.

The door opened anyway, and a wedge of hallway light fell across his floor. His father's silhouette filled the doorway, broader than Marcus remembered. When had he last really looked at his dad?

"Can I come in?"

Marcus shrugged, even though his father probably couldn't see it in the darkness.

His father entered, closing the door behind him. The mattress dipped as he sat on the edge of Marcus's bed, and Marcus caught the familiar scent of coffee and the faint chemical smell of the print shop that always clung to his father's clothes, no matter how many times they were washed.

They sat in silence. Marcus counted his heartbeats—twenty-three, twenty-four, twenty-five—waiting for the lecture, the disappointment, the inevitable "What were you thinking?"

Instead, his father said, "I saw the setup you built. In the garage."

Marcus's chest tightened. "I know it was stupid."

"That's not what I was going to say." His father's voice was quiet, thoughtful. "The heating lamp was positioned at exactly the right height. You researched the proper temperature range, didn't you? And the feeding schedule on the wall—every three hours. Even through the night."

Marcus blinked in the darkness, confused. "Dad—"

"You built him a climbing structure from those old shelves. Added padding to all the sharp corners." His father paused. "When did you sleep?"

"I... I didn't. Not much." Marcus's voice cracked. "I had to make sure he was okay. He was so small when I found him, and he was crying, and nobody was coming for him, and—" The words tumbled out faster now, pressure building behind them like water against a dam. "I know it was wrong. I know I should have called someone right away. But Dad, he needed me. Someone needed me."

The last words came out as barely a whisper.

His father was quiet for a long moment. Then: "Turn on your lamp."

Marcus hesitated before reaching over and clicking on the small reading light beside his bed. Warm yellow light filled the space between them, and for the first time, Marcus saw his father's face. Really saw it.

His dad looked tired. Not just end-of-day tired, but bone-deep exhausted. There were new lines around his eyes that Marcus didn't remember, gray threading through his dark hair at the temples. But it was his expression that made Marcus's throat tighten—raw and open, like he'd taken off armor he'd been wearing for years.

"Tell me about feeling invisible," his father said.

Marcus's breath caught. "What?"

"You said someone needed you. Like you didn't think..." His father's jaw worked, searching for words. "Like you didn't think anyone here did."

"I didn't mean—" Marcus started, but his father held up a hand.

"Please. I need you to be honest with me. I'm listening. Really listening."

Something in Marcus's chest cracked open. All the words he'd been swallowing for months—years maybe—suddenly pressed against his lips, demanding release.

"You're never here," Marcus said, and his voice shook. "I know you're working. I know you're trying to keep the print shop going and you're stressed about money and Mom's worried all the time. I know all that. But Dad, I'm here too. I'm right here, and it's like..." He gestured helplessly at his fading stars. "It's like I'm disappearing. Like I'm becoming see-through."

His father's face crumpled, just for a second, before he steadied himself.

"At school, I'm just Marcus-who-doesn't-talk. At home, I'm just the kid who's fine, who doesn't need anything, who can take care of himself." Marcus's hands twisted in his comforter. "And then I found Bruno, and he was so helpless, and when I held him, he looked at me like I was the most important person in the world. Like I mattered. Like if I disappeared, it would actually mean something to someone."

"Marcus—"

"I know it was selfish!" The words burst out louder than Marcus intended. "I know I put him in danger by keeping him. I know I scared Mom and broke about a million laws and probably traumatized a baby bear because I was too stupid and selfish to do the right thing. But for three days, Dad, for three days I felt like I had a purpose. Like someone needed me to show up. To be there. To not disappear."

Tears were streaming down Marcus's face now, hot and fast. His father reached out, and Marcus thought he might ruffle his hair or pat his shoulder—the distant, awkward gestures he'd grown used to. Instead, his father pulled him into a fierce hug, arms tight around Marcus's shoulders.

"I'm sorry," his father said, voice thick. "God, Marcus, I'm so sorry."

Marcus froze, then collapsed into the embrace, sobbing into his father's shoulder. His father held him tighter, one hand cupping the back of Marcus's head like he was something precious and breakable.

"I thought I was doing the right thing," his father said. "Working extra hours, taking on more clients. Trying to provide. But I was so focused on giving you things—a house, food, stability—that I forgot to give you me. I forgot to show up."

"I don't need things," Marcus choked out. "I just need you to see me."

"I see you." His father pulled back just enough to look Marcus in the eyes. His own eyes were red-rimmed, wet. "I see you, son. And I'm sorry it took a bear in our garage for me to realize I'd been looking right through you."

They sat there in the small circle of lamplight, the house quiet around them. Marcus's breathing gradually steadied, his father's hand still resting on his shoulder.

"What you did for Bruno," his father said finally, "keeping him alive, caring for him around the clock—that took real dedication. Real love." He paused. "But you know we can't keep him."

Marcus nodded, fresh tears threatening. "I know. He deserves better than a garage. Better than me."

"No." His father's voice was firm. "He deserves what's best for him. And so do you. That's not the same as saying you're not enough." He squeezed Marcus's shoulder. "What if we could find a way to do right by Bruno and by you? What if we worked on this together?"

Marcus looked up, hope flickering in his chest like a candle in the wind. "Together?"

"Together." His father stood, moving to Marcus's desk and opening the laptop there. The screen's blue glow joined the lamplight. "I did some research after dinner. There are wildlife sanctuaries that specialize in orphaned bears. Places where Bruno could get proper medical care and eventually live with other bears in a natural habitat."

"Really?" Marcus scrambled off the bed to stand beside his father.

"Really." His father pulled up a website showing sprawling forested enclosures and rehabilitation facilities. "It's not a quick fix. We still have to apologize to the zoo, face whatever consequences come from this. And Bruno still has to leave. But maybe..." He looked at Marcus. "Maybe we can make sure he goes somewhere that gives him the best possible life. And maybe you could be part of that process."

Marcus stared at the screen, at the images of bears climbing trees and splashing in streams. It wasn't keeping Bruno. It wasn't the fantasy he'd built in his head of somehow making this work. But it was something real. Something better.

# **Scene 4: Marcus, his father, and zoo officials meet at the house to discuss Bruno's future**

The dining room had never felt so formal. Mom had cleared the table of its usual clutter—the stack of unread magazines, Dad's laptop, the basket of mail—and set out a pitcher of water with actual glasses instead of their everyday plastic cups. Marcus sat between his parents, his hands folded in his lap, still wearing the same clothes from yesterday. He hadn't slept.

Through the window, he could see the garage. Bruno was in there, sedated by the veterinarian who'd come last night. Safe. Sleeping. For now.

The doorbell rang, and Marcus's stomach dropped.

"I'll get it," Dad said, squeezing Marcus's shoulder as he stood.

Marcus listened to the murmur of voices in the hallway—his father's low rumble, a woman's professional tone, a man's softer response. Then they appeared in the doorway: a woman in her forties with kind eyes and graying hair pulled back in a practical bun, and a younger man carrying a tablet and a leather portfolio.

"Marcus, this is Dr. Chen, the zoo's head veterinarian," Dad said, gesturing to the woman. "And Mr. Rodriguez, the director of animal welfare."

"Hello, Marcus." Dr. Chen's voice was warm but serious as she took a seat across from him. Mr. Rodriguez sat beside her, placing his portfolio on the table with a soft thump that made Marcus flinch.

"Thank you for meeting with us," Mom said, her voice tight. She'd been crying on and off since yesterday, and her eyes were still red-rimmed.

Dr. Chen poured herself a glass of water, the liquid catching the morning light streaming through the window. "Before we begin, I want to say something." She looked directly at Marcus. "What you did was dangerous and illegal. You put yourself, your family, and the cub at risk."

Marcus nodded, unable to meet her eyes.

"But," she continued, and something in her tone made him look up, "in my twenty years working with animals, I've rarely seen a wild animal in unauthorized care look as healthy as Bruno does. You clearly researched his needs. You kept him clean, well-fed, and from what I can tell, relatively stress-free."

Marcus felt his throat tighten. "I tried. I just—I wanted him to be okay."

"He is okay," Mr. Rodriguez said, opening his portfolio. "Which is why we're here to discuss options rather than simply reclaiming him."

Options. The word hung in the air like a lifeline.

Dad leaned forward. "Marcus and I did some research last night. We found information about wildlife rehabilitation sanctuaries that specialize in orphaned bears."

Dr. Chen's eyebrows rose. "You're thinking of the Northern Wildlife Sanctuary?"

"Yes," Marcus said, his voice cracking with sudden hope. "They have a program for cubs who can't be released into the wild. They—they live in natural habitats with other bears. They get proper medical care and—"

"And they're not in cages," Mr. Rodriguez finished gently. "I know the sanctuary well. We've worked with them before."

Marcus's heart hammered against his ribs. "Could Bruno go there? Instead of back to the zoo?"

Dr. Chen and Mr. Rodriguez exchanged a look that Marcus couldn't quite read. The silence stretched out, broken only by the ticking of the wall clock and the distant hum of the refrigerator.

"The zoo's bear exhibit is designed for adult bears," Dr. Chen said finally. "Bruno's mother was pregnant when she arrived, and we weren't prepared for a cub. Our facilities aren't ideal for raising an orphaned bear to maturity."

"The sanctuary would be better," Mr. Rodriguez added. "But there are protocols. Paperwork. The sanctuary would need to accept him, and there are costs involved."

"We'll pay," Dad said immediately. "Whatever it takes."

Mom reached across the table and grabbed Dad's hand. "We will," she agreed, her voice thick with emotion.

Mr. Rodriguez pulled out his tablet and began typing. "Let me contact the sanctuary director. If they have space in their cub program, and if Dr. Chen clears Bruno medically for transport, we could potentially arrange a transfer within the week."

Within the week. Marcus felt dizzy with the speed of it all. Yesterday, Bruno had been his secret. Today, people were discussing his future like adults planning something important. Something real.

"Marcus," Dr. Chen said, pulling his attention back. "If this works out, the sanctuary has a volunteer program for young people. You'd need to complete their training, follow their rules, and understand that Bruno will become wilder as he bonds with other cubs. But you could visit. Help with his care."

"Really?" The word came out as barely a whisper.

"Really." She smiled, and it softened her whole face. "You made a mistake, but you also showed remarkable dedication. The sanctuary values that kind of commitment."

Marcus looked at his father, who was watching him with an expression Marcus had never seen before—something like pride mixed with sadness and understanding.

"There's one condition," Mr. Rodriguez said, his tone becoming more serious. "You'll need to complete a youth education program about wildlife conservation and why wild animals can't be pets. And you'll have community service hours at the zoo, helping us educate other kids about proper animal care."

"I'll do it," Marcus said immediately. "Anything. I just—I want what's best for Bruno."

"Then we're in agreement." Mr. Rodriguez closed his portfolio. "I'll make the calls this afternoon. Dr. Chen will examine Bruno again and prepare the medical documentation. Marcus, you and your parents should visit the sanctuary's website, read about their program, and prepare yourselves for what this transition will look like."

Dr. Chen stood, and everyone else followed. "One more thing, Marcus. When it's time to transport Bruno, you can come along. You can help settle him into his new home. But after that, the sanctuary staff will take over his primary care. You understand?"

Marcus nodded, his eyes burning with unshed tears. "I understand. He needs to be with other bears. He needs to be wild."

"He does," Dr. Chen agreed softly. "But that doesn't mean you didn't love him. It means you loved him enough to want what's right for him."

After the zoo officials left, Marcus sat at the dining room table while his parents cleaned up the water glasses. Through the window, the garage stood silent in the morning sun. Inside, Bruno was sleeping, unaware that his whole world was about to change again.

But this time, Marcus thought, it would change for the better. This time, Bruno would have space to run, other bears to play with, and people who knew how to help him grow into what he was meant to be.

"Marcus," Dad said, sitting back down beside him. "I'm proud of you."

Marcus looked at his father, surprised. "For stealing a bear cub?"

"For loving something enough to let it go." Dad's voice was rough with emotion. "That takes real courage."

Mom sat down on Marcus's other side and wrapped her arm around his shoulders. "We're going to get through this together," she said. "All of us."

For the first time since his mother had screamed in the garage yesterday, Marcus felt like he could breathe. Bruno would be okay. He would be okay. And maybe, just maybe, his family would be okay too.

Outside, a bird sang in the maple tree, and somewhere in the garage, a bear cub dreamed of forests he'd never seen but would soon call home.

# **Scene 5: Marcus helps transport Bruno to the sanctuary and spends the day learning about the facility, meeting the other orphaned cubs, and beginning to understand that this is where Bruno truly belongs.**

The carrier rattled softly in the back of Dad's truck as they turned onto the gravel road. Marcus sat twisted in his seat, one hand pressed against the rear window, watching Bruno's dark shape shift behind the ventilation slots.

"He's okay," Dad said quietly. "We're almost there."

Marcus nodded but didn't turn around. His throat felt tight, had felt tight since they'd coaxed Bruno into the carrier two hours ago with pieces of apple and peanut butter. Bruno had gone willingly, trusting, and that somehow made everything worse.

The Wildwood Wildlife Rehabilitation Sanctuary appeared gradually through the trees—first a weathered wooden sign, then a sprawling property with multiple buildings connected by covered walkways. Nothing like the concrete and steel of the zoo. Here, everything seemed designed to blend into the forest surrounding it.

A woman with silver-streaked hair pulled back in a braid stood waiting in the parking area. She wore cargo pants and a fleece jacket with the sanctuary logo, and when she smiled, the lines around her eyes deepened.

"You must be Marcus," she said, extending her hand. "I'm Sarah. Welcome to Wildwood."

Marcus shook her hand, surprised by the firmness of her grip. Behind her, another figure emerged from one of the buildings—Jade, the veterinary tech from the zoo. She'd changed out of her uniform into jeans and a flannel shirt, but her expression was the same mixture of kindness and professional concern.

"Hey, Marcus," Jade said. "Thought you might like a familiar face today."

Something loosened in Marcus's chest. "You work here too?"

"Volunteer on weekends. This is where I spend most of my free time, actually." She gestured toward the carrier. "How's our patient doing?"

Together, they unloaded Bruno. Dad and Sarah carried the front handles while Marcus and Jade took the rear. Bruno made a soft huffing sound, and Marcus could see one dark paw pressed against the carrier's side.

"Easy, little one," Sarah murmured. "Almost home."

They carried him past the main building into a climate-controlled nursery that smelled of fresh hay and something sweet Marcus couldn't identify. Soft classical music played from hidden speakers. Three other enclosures lined the walls, each one spacious and filled with climbing structures, toys, and comfortable bedding.

In the far enclosure, two bear cubs wrestled over a rubber ball, tumbling over each other in a tangle of brown fur. They were slightly larger than Bruno, healthier-looking, their coats glossy under the warm lights.

"Those are Maple and Birch," Sarah explained as they set Bruno's carrier down. "Sisters. Found them last spring after their mother was hit by a car. They'll be Bruno's neighbors for a while, until he's strong enough to join them."

Marcus watched the cubs play, something aching in his chest. He'd never been able to give Bruno this—other bears, space to run, proper care.

Sarah opened the carrier door carefully, stepping back to give Bruno room. For a long moment, nothing happened. Then Bruno's black nose appeared, sniffing cautiously. He emerged slowly, limping slightly on his injured leg, eyes wide and uncertain.

The room went silent except for the music and the soft sounds of Maple and Birch playing. Bruno took three tentative steps, then stopped, turning back toward the carrier—toward Marcus.

"It's okay," Marcus whispered, kneeling down. His voice cracked. "It's okay, Bruno."

Bruno made a sound Marcus had never heard before, somewhere between a whimper and a question. The other cubs stopped playing, pressing against their enclosure wall to watch the newcomer.

Jade crouched beside Marcus. "See those two? They were just like Bruno when they arrived. Scared, hurt, not sure if they'd ever feel safe again." She pointed to where Maple had her paw on Birch's back, the two of them watching with curious eyes. "Now look at them."

Bruno took another step forward, then another. He approached a water bowl, sniffed it, then drank deeply. When he finished, he looked around the enclosure with what Marcus could only describe as wonder—at the climbing log, the pile of enrichment toys, the soft bedding in the corner.

"He's already exploring," Sarah said softly. "That's a very good sign."

Dad's hand settled on Marcus's shoulder. "You kept him alive long enough to get here. That's what matters."
